REFLECTIONS

As I mentioned on the Process page, the methods and data feasibly available to research greyfield properties--especially to an undergraduate student--are fairly limited!

In the social sciences, a couple of decades isn't a terribly long time. As a result, for all intents and purposes, the concept of a greyfield was introduced to the urban planning consciousness fairly recently. This meant that there wasn't a large body of literature or policy history relating to them that I could use for preliminary research, and that wasn't a consistent definition of what, exactly, a greyfield is out there for me to use to define variables. Thus, the basic criteria for a greyfield were defined by myself, with the help of Professor Hurley and Lee Sobel, the author of Greyfields into Goldfields and a professional to whom this project owes an enormous debt. While I took pains to be precise with the criteria, they are ultimately only an approximation of a larger set of constantly-evolving definitions.

My criteria and the subject of the project--Fairfax County and City--were also heavily influenced by the data available to me. Greyfields as an idea hinge on the underuse of land, which is a slippery concept to define and even harder to document. First, it carries the assumption that land is something to fundamentally be used for something "productive", which necessitates its own debate; however, this assumption is absolutely crucial to the planning field. Secondly, it's very difficult to draw lines around "underuse": what counts as underuse, how do you tell if land meets criteria, and how long must underuse continue to necessitate some kind of intervention to return a piece of land to use, if intervention is necessary or right at all? Due to these more abstract considerations as well as concerns about the privacy of landowners and the sheer amount of money, technology, and manpower it would take to keep records about land underuse or abandonment, it's extremely difficult to find any kind of solid quantitative record of whether or not a piece of land is, in fact, being used to some degree. Utility shutoffs were my closest approximation of a criterion that, in many cases, approximates active abandonment of a property more than just underuse. On top of this, the frequency of unavailable data in utility connection tends to reduce its reliability--after all, there is a chance those null values stood in place of a utility that was, in fact, connected. Additionally, I specifically chose Fairfax as the subject of my research due to the county and city's remarkable data transparency and the level of detail in geospatial data they make available to the public.

The logistics of data management over the course of the project were not only a constant headache for me, but also an example of the way data transfer can limit data organization and cause errors. A lost record or mismatched file can spell doom for a researcher trying to make sure their results are as accurate and transparent as possible, and this was a common problem that forced me to stop research and re-do analysis steps while I data cleaned, joined tables, organized files across different storage locations, and transformed data into a variety of formats several times over.

Additionally, some statistical mishaps occurred due to human error! When weighting block groups using the Suitaibility Index, I failed to consider that most of the variables I was using (listed in step 4 of the Process page) are highly correlated.

As a result of the above limitations, my method wasn't 100% accurate (you can find more details on this at the bottom of the Process page) and is more than open to improvement from others seeking to take another look at greyfields in Fairfax or other areas.

There are many different future directions in which this project could have gone if I'd had more time. With the opportunity to take a more in-depth look at the subject of greyfields, I likely would've made the following part of my project.

  • An expansion of scale, taking a look at other suburban counties in Virginia and the United States.
  • The incorporation of satellite imagery into analysis by using machine learning tools to indicate potential greyfield properties identified in orthoimagery.
  • Addition of different network analysis problem sets into my primary analysis in order to tailor the project to more audiences with potential greyfield property selection criteria driven by interests other than nonprofit community development, such as property developers.
  • More in-depth analysis of greyfield distribution using a wider variety of demographic variables from the Census and other sources.

Because I didn't have the opportunity myself, I invite anyone interested to take those directions!

It's impossible to overstate how much I learned by doing this project! This is one of the hardest and most rewarding projects I've worked on over the course of my college career so far, and I'm very pleased with the hard and soft skills that I gained through my work on it:

  • Additional project management skills and experience, particularly in the development and completion of a several months-long project that was created to be presented on a publicly-available website.
  • Familiarity with the following in ArcGIS Pro:
    • suitability index creation,
    • network analysis dataset creation,
    • use of network analysis tools,
    • connection to ArcGIS Online,
    • exporting of raster data,
    • and the Tabulate Intersection, Raster to Polygon, Summarize Within, and Feature to Point operations.
  • Experience in data management and transparency through data dictionaries and record keeping about processes.
  • The creation of a basic functioning website using HTML and CSS.
  • Communication with professionals working in the field in order to clarify research.
  • Use of online resources, ranging from YouTube tutorials to the longest Stack Overflow threads you've ever seen.
  • Persistence, a lot of patience, and the ability to accept that not every part of an exploratory project is going to go somewhere.

While I still learned something valuable during every wrong turn I took, there were some very notable wrong turns over the course of the project that ultimately didn't feature in the final analysis:

  • an attempt to use deep learning to identify potential greyfield properties using empty parking lots via a lack of cars on the property indicating lack of use (scrapped due to issues with the download of ESRI's Deep Learning Libraries, the accuracy of the model, chance that cars simply weren't on the property at the time the orthoimage was taken);
  • an attempt to use deep learning to identify impermeable surface from orthoimagery more recent than the 2017 Impermeable Surface data available from the county (scrapped due to issues with the download of Esri's Deep Learning Libraries and glitches blocking use of the Classification Wizard)
  • an attempt to create a network dataset of my own using OpenStreetMap data before figuring out how to use credits to use the ArcGIS Online network dataset, resulting in a 2-week fight with, in order:
    • Importing shapefiles from BBBike Extracts of the OSM network
    • Importing and converting Geodatabases from Geofabrik
    • Downloading OSM data available from Esri
    • Using the OSMQuery tool created by riccardoklinger on GitHub to import data directly into ArcPro
    • Importing OSM data into QGIS and saving it as a shapefile
    • My Geodata Cloud converter
    • Street Data Processing Tools by ESRI;
  • and an attempt to add public school rating data from the website Niche as another socioeconomic status indicator for block groups in the county (scrapped due to lack of access to the Niche API and time constraints that prevented me from manually copying down the school rating data).

Additionally, apart from all of the things listed above that went wrong, there were many other challenges I faced over the course of the project.

  • I went into January without a mentor and several different possible projects in mind; that meant a lot of time that could've been used to get started with a project was spent brainstorming and outlining goals that I could've already had in mind at the beginning of the semester.
  • I'm happy with the skills I learned through the creation of this website, but I wish I hadn't made it the vehicle for my final product: it was extremely time-consuming to make, as I had never made a website using HTML and CSS before, and I wish I had spent that time on polishing my data and other sub-projects that developed my GIS analysis skills instead of the presentation of my analysis.
  • I only started taking notes on the operations I used in the analysis and key variables in each dataset halfway and a third of the way through the semester respectively, which meant I had to spend some time digging through my ArcGIS Pro history in order to piece together things I had done and later forgotten for the Process page and Data Dictionary.
  • This class was taken concurrently with other very labor-intensive self-directed classes, which made this semester a crash course in time management.
  • To top everything off, ArcGIS Online doesn't allow you to represent the levels of your data in percentages. I hope you enjoy the decimals.

Despite these challenges, I'm ultimately very pleased with the progress I made over the course of the semester and am very grateful that I had the opportunity to take the class!
